Prototyping and Testing

Design Review

The design and prototyping process for creating accessible door accessories has primarily been testing existing models found online and interviewing some users in one of our target demographics to understand their likes and dislikes of the existing solutions.

In our interviews, we received many pieces of feedback, positive and negative, all helping us refine our idea. One of our primary takeaways from these interviews was that users did not want to have something that needs to be put on and taken off every time they want to open the door. However, having something portable would be nice in situations where the door knobs are sticky and the doors are heavy.

Another takeaway from this process was that being able to open a door with your elbow is very useful—not just for those with mobility issues, but also when your hands are full. From this feedback, we decided to make sure the handle would stick out far enough to ensure a person could apply sufficient pulling force to turn and open the door.

Not all of the feedback was positive. In one interview, we were told the handle did not feel secure on the door. This is particularly important, as the handle needs to be able to secure to all kinds of door knobs while still being easily removable when necessary. Another comment about the stability of the handle was less about its attachment to the door and more about the security when grabbing it. It was suggested we add a curve to the end of the handle, making an almost L-shape, to ensure a hand cannot slide off while twisting it. This shape would also provide a bumper for elbow operation.

Overall, the feedback we received was very helpful in furthering our design and our understanding of the problem at hand.

We made some changes to our How Might We statements while collecting and analyzing feedback on our initial prototype. We found that our initial design already fit several different door knob shapes, but a more pressing issue was that it could be easily pulled or pushed out of place.

We also decided that the durability of metal screws outweighed the concern of accessibility of materials, since most students at Brandeis who would possibly be 3D printing this design would already be in a maker space to do so. In this case, we would want to ensure that those spaces were stocked with the additional materials needed to assemble our door knob adapter.

Additionally, we still want to work with DCL to ensure that our design is safe and compliant with any rules and regulations. If possible, we want to make it an option to semi-permanently install this design in your dorm room! The last change we made was a shift towards durability. Whether by using a stronger material or modifying our original design to be more sturdy, we want to ensure that these adapters can be used for an extended period of time if required.

Problem Definition, Brainstorming, and Storyboarding

Problem Definition

Door knobs are a common fixture found in many of the dorms and buildings on Brandeis campus, however they can be difficult to use for people with wrist injuries. This project aims to create a solution that will make it easier for people with wrist injuries and mobility issues to open doors.

Thematic Analysis

Our thematic analysis resulted in 7 categories of ideas. These ideas allowed us to continue refining our final design to the following insight statements.

Insight Statements

  1. Door knobs are included in both residential and academic building design, but are not universal between buildings.
  2. People with wrist injuries or limited arm movement prefer pushing motions over twisting/pulling.
  3. People with access to 3D printers may or may not have access to the correct hardware to supplement our various design ideas.
  4. Many people appreciate door designs that avoid germ transmission/don’t require the use of hands to open.
  5. It is important to include access to a keyhole for door knobs with locking mechanisms.
  6. Interviewees expressed interest in a semi-permanently installed solution over a portable system.
  7. It is important for the doorknob to work on either side of a door, as well as on differently shaped door knobs.

Brainstorming

Final Idea

Our final idea is an attachment for a standard door knob that can be 3d printed and assembled with standard, off the shelf hardware to convert a door knob to a lever-style door handle. Our design will allow for easy installation and removable and will comply with the safety regulations of the state of Massachusetts.

Storyboarding

Interviews

Interviewee 1

Brandeis student from New Jersey who has injured their wrist in the past. Their injury came from past rock climbing and volleyball incidents that have resulted in lasting pain and mobility problems. Due to their wrist injury and residual effects (such as pain), they struggle to open door knobs with their injured hand. The solutions they had found in their daily life have been using their other (non-dominant) hand, and preferring doors with levers or push operation.

Interviewee 2

Brandeis student from Massachusetts with a history of shoulder injuries from various athletic pursuits. (Dodgeball, volleyball, quadball) Has had 2 surgeries to prevent future dislocations of the shoulder. They don’t find door knobs too inconvenient, but agreed that push doors and handles were definitely preferable options.

Interviewee 3

Brandeis rugby player who injured their wrist over time while playing. They have not been to a doctor, but notice their injury getting worse when falling/tackling and catching. They have door knobs in their dorm, and will often use their non-dominant hand (not injured) to open doors to avoid pain.
